# AlphaGo: Deep Neural Networks and Tree Search

> **Primary citation:** Silver, D., Huang, A., Maddison, C. J., Guez, A., Sifre, L., van den Driessche, G., … Hassabis, D. (2016). Mastering the game of Go with deep neural networks and tree search. *Nature*, **529**(7587), 484–489. DOI: [10.1038/nature16961](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature16961). PDF: [AlphaGoNaturePaper.pdf](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/alphago/AlphaGoNaturePaper.pdf)

---

## 1. The Challenge of Go

Go has a branching factor *b* ≈ 250 and game depth *d* ≈ 150 moves, yielding a search space on the order of 10^170 board configurations — vastly beyond the reach of exhaustive search or traditional alpha–beta pruning. Prior computer-Go programs reached only amateur ~5-dan level. AlphaGo's core insight is to simultaneously **reduce the breadth** of the search tree (via a *policy network* that focuses on plausible moves) and **reduce its depth** (via a *value network* that evaluates positions without rollouts to terminal states).

---

## 2. Policy Network Architecture (SL Policy Network, p_σ)

**Architecture:** A **13-layer convolutional neural network** (CNN) with rectifier non-linearities between layers. The board is encoded as a **19×19 image** with multiple input feature planes capturing: stone positions, liberties, capture counts, ko points, and several domain-specific features (e.g., nakade patterns, ladder status). The final layer is a softmax producing a probability distribution over all legal moves.

**Training data:** 29.4 million board positions from **160,000 KGS (Kiseido Go Server) games**, each augmented with all 8 dihedral symmetries (rotations and reflections), yielding an effective dataset of ~30 million positions.

**Training procedure:** Asynchronous SGD maximising log-likelihood of the expert's move. Learning rate initialised at 0.003, halved every 80 million steps; mini-batch size = 16.

**Performance:** Predicted expert moves with **57% accuracy** — a significant jump over prior CNN approaches. Small accuracy improvements translated into large Elo gains.

### Fast Rollout Policy (p_π)

A separate, lightweight **linear softmax** over local pattern-based features. Trained on 8 million positions from human games. Achieves **24.2% move accuracy** but executes in ~**2 μs per move** (vs. ~3 ms for the full policy network). Used during MCTS rollouts to simulate game-endings quickly.

---

## 3. Value Network Architecture (v_θ)

**Architecture:** Identical CNN structure to the SL/RL policy networks (13 convolutional layers), but with a **single scalar output** (instead of a softmax over moves). The scalar represents the expected outcome of the game (win probability) from the current position under the RL policy network's self-play.

**Key difference from the policy network:** The policy network outputs a *distribution over moves* (which action to take); the value network outputs a *single scalar* (how good is this board position). An additional input plane encodes whose turn it is to play.

**Training data:** A purpose-built dataset of **30 million distinct positions**, each sampled from a *separate self-play game* to prevent overfitting from adjacent, highly correlated positions. Each position was generated by:
1. Sampling a random time-step *U* in a game
2. Playing moves 1 to U−1 with the SL policy network
3. Playing one move uniformly at random
4. Playing the remainder of the game with the RL policy network until termination
5. Recording the final outcome (+1 win / −1 loss) as the training label

**Training objective:** Regression (MSE) on final game outcomes. MSE = 0.226 (train), 0.234 (test) — indicating good generalisation without overfitting.

**Efficiency:** A single evaluation of the value network approached the accuracy of **Monte Carlo rollouts using the full RL policy network** but required **15,000× less computation**.

---

## 4. Training Pipeline

The full training pipeline has **three sequential stages:**

### Stage 1 — Supervised Learning (SL) of the Policy Network
- Train p_σ directly from human expert moves (KGS games)
- Initialises the system with human-level intuitions
- Provides high-quality gradients and fast learning

### Stage 2 — Reinforcement Learning (RL) of the Policy Network (p_ρ)
- Initialise p_ρ with weights from p_σ (same 13-layer architecture)
- Play games between p_ρ and a randomly-selected **previous iteration** of p_ρ (pool of opponents, to prevent overfitting to a single fixed opponent)
- Reward signal: +1 for a win, −1 for a loss at game termination
- Update via **REINFORCE** policy gradient with value network baseline for variance reduction
- Training: 10,000 minibatches of 128 games

**Results:** The RL policy network won >**80% of games against the SL policy network** in head-to-head play, and won **85% of games against Pachi** (a strong MCTS-based open-source engine at ~2-amateur-dan), using *no lookahead search*. Prior CNN-only approaches won only ~11% against Pachi.

### Stage 3 — Training the Value Network (v_θ)
- Use the RL policy network to generate self-play data
- Construct the decorrelated 30M-position dataset (described above)
- Train v_θ via regression on game outcomes

## 5. MCTS Integration: APV-MCTS

AlphaGo uses an **Asynchronous Policy and Value Monte Carlo Tree Search (APV-MCTS)** algorithm. This is the key integration mechanism that combines the trained networks with lookahead search.

### Tree Structure
Each edge (s, a) stores:
- **P(s, a)** — prior probability from the SL policy network
- **Q(s, a)** — combined mean action value (blend of value network + rollout)
- **N(s, a)** — visit count

### Four Phases of APV-MCTS

**1. Selection**
Starting from the root, traverse the tree by selecting at each node the action *a* maximising:
$$a_t = \arg\max_a \left[ Q(s_t, a) + u(s_t, a) \right]$$
where *u(s, a)* is a UCB-style bonus proportional to P(s, a) / (1 + N(s, a)), encouraging exploration of high-prior, less-visited edges.

**2. Expansion**
When a leaf node *s_L* is reached, the **SL policy network** (not the RL network) computes prior probabilities P(s_L, a) for all legal moves. A successor node is added to the tree when its visit count exceeds a dynamic threshold (tuned so the rate of node addition matches GPU evaluation throughput). Mini-batch size = 1 for minimum latency.

> Note: The SL network (not RL) is used for priors during MCTS. Empirically, SL priors led to better game play than RL priors, because human moves are more *diverse* and serve as better exploration guidance.

**3. Evaluation**
Two evaluations happen at the leaf:
- The **value network** v_θ evaluates the position (asynchronously on GPU)
- The **fast rollout policy** p_π simulates the game to completion from the leaf, producing a Monte Carlo estimate of the outcome

The final evaluation is a **weighted combination**:
$$V(s_L) = (1 - \lambda) \cdot v_\theta(s_L) + \lambda \cdot z_L$$
where z_L is the rollout outcome and λ is a mixing parameter (λ = 0.5 in the paper).

**4. Backup**
Statistics Q(s, a) and N(s, a) are updated for all edges traversed. Virtual losses are applied during search to discourage other threads from exploring identical paths simultaneously.

### Asynchronous Implementation
- **40 search threads** run simulations on **CPUs**
- Policy and value networks evaluated in parallel on **GPUs**
- Distributed version: policy evaluation fanned across **worker GPUs**; in-tree simulation on **worker CPUs**

## 6. Computational Requirements

| Configuration | Threads | CPUs | GPUs | Elo |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Single machine | 40 | 48 | 1 | 2,181 |
| Single machine | 40 | 48 | 8 | 2,890 |
| Distributed | 12 | 428 | 64 | 2,937 |
| Distributed | 24 | 764 | 112 | 3,079 |
| **Distributed (vs. Fan Hui)** | **40** | **1,202** | **176** | **3,140** |
| Distributed | 64 | 1,920 | 280 | 3,168 |

*Source: Silver et al. (2016), Table S5; Wikipedia AlphaGo article.*

The **distributed version** used for the Fan Hui match ran with **1,202 CPUs** and **176 GPUs**. The version used against Lee Sedol ran on Google's cloud infrastructure and according to *The Economist* used **1,920 CPUs and 280 GPUs** (Demis Hassabis noted it used "roughly the same amount of compute" as the Fan Hui match in a tweet, suggesting comparable scale). Google later disclosed that AlphaGo Lee also used **48 TPUs** (tensor processing units).

## 7. Match Results

### vs. Fan Hui — October 2015, London (DeepMind HQ)
- **Result: AlphaGo 5 – 0 Fan Hui**
- Fan Hui: European Go Champion, 2-dan professional
- First time in history a computer Go program defeated a professional player on a full 19×19 board without handicap
- The match was held in secret; results were embargoed until 27 January 2016 to coincide with the *Nature* paper publication
- AlphaGo used the distributed version (1,202 CPUs, 176 GPUs)
- Fan Hui subsequently joined DeepMind as a Go consultant

> Source: [AlphaGo versus Fan Hui — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlphaGo_versus_Fan_Hui)

### vs. Lee Sedol — March 2016, Seoul (Four Seasons Hotel)
- **Result: AlphaGo 4 – 1 Lee Sedol**
- Lee Sedol: 9-dan professional, one of the strongest players in the world (second-highest international championship victories at the time)
- Five games played: 9, 10, 12, 13, and 15 March 2016; livestreamed globally; watched by ~100 million people
- First time a computer defeated a 9-dan professional without handicap
- Games 1–3: AlphaGo won by resignation
- Game 4: Lee Sedol won by resignation at move 180 — the only human victory against AlphaGo across all 74 official games. Lee's move 78 ("the divine move") exploited a weakness in AlphaGo's policy network guidance that caused mis-evaluation
- Game 5: AlphaGo won by resignation
- Prize: $1 million (donated to UNICEF and other charities); Lee received $150,000 participation fee + $20,000 for his Game 4 win
- AlphaGo was awarded honorary **9-dan** by the Korea Baduk Association
- Rules: Chinese rules, 7.5-point komi, 2h thinking time + 3×60s byoyomi

> Source: [AlphaGo versus Lee Sedol — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlphaGo_versus_Lee_Sedol); Silver et al. (2016)

## 8. AlphaGo Master (Late 2016 – 2017)

Following the Lee Sedol match, DeepMind developed a substantially improved version of AlphaGo, subsequently revealed to be **AlphaGo Master**.

### Online Blitz Matches (December 2016 – January 2017)
- Starting **29 December 2016**, an account named "Magister" (later "Master") appeared on the Tygem and FoxGo servers
- Played at 10 games/day, with only 20–30 second byo-yomi
- DeepMind confirmed on **4 January 2017** that "Master" was AlphaGo
- Final record: **60 wins – 0 losses** against top professional players worldwide
- Opponents included world-ranked players: **Ke Jie** (×3 wins), Yuta Iyama, Park Jeong-hwan, Gu Li, Mi Yuting, Shi Yue, Chen Yaoye, and many others
- Gu Li offered a 100,000 yuan (≈ $14,400) bounty for any human who could defeat Master (unclaimed)
- After game 59, Aja Huang (DeepMind) revealed himself in the chat as the human operator
- Ke Jie's reaction: *"After humanity spent thousands of years improving our tactics, computers tell us that humans are completely wrong... I would go as far as to say not a single human has touched the edge of the truth of Go."*

> Sources: [Wikipedia AlphaGo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlphaGo); [CCTV News](https://english.cctv.com/2017/01/06/ARTIgmOBQuMAbZLmR9XIR9Qf170106.shtml); [Ars Technica](https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2017/01/alphago-is-back-and-secretly-crushing-the-worlds-best-human-players/)

### Future of Go Summit — May 2017, Wuzhen, China
- AlphaGo Master played Ke Jie (world #1) in a 3-game formal match
- **Result: AlphaGo Master 3 – 0 Ke Jie**
- Also played pair Go and team games with top Chinese professionals
- AlphaGo Master awarded **professional 9-dan** by the Chinese Weiqi Association
- After the Summit, DeepMind retired AlphaGo and disbanded the dedicated Go team
- DeepMind released 50 AlphaGo vs. AlphaGo self-play games as a gift to the Go community

### Hardware (AlphaGo Master)
- Ran on a **single machine with 4 TPUs** (Tensor Processing Units)
- Elo rating: ~4,858 — compared to AlphaGo Lee's ~3,739 and AlphaGo Fan's ~3,144

## 10. Significance and Legacy

- AlphaGo's victory arrived ~**10 years earlier** than most experts expected
- Lee Sedol is the **only human to beat AlphaGo** in official play; he retired in November 2019, citing AI dominance
- AlphaGo was selected as a *Science* **Breakthrough of the Year** runner-up (2016)
- The AlphaGo team received the inaugural **IJCAI Marvin Minsky Medal** (2017)
- AlphaGo triggered China's "Sputnik moment" — the Lee Sedol match directly influenced China's decision to dramatically increase AI funding
- The methodology (MCTS + deep neural networks) has since been applied to drug discovery, protein folding, and many other domains
- AlphaGo Zero (2017) then surpassed AlphaGo Lee **100-0** using only self-play, without any human game data

## Gaps / Open Questions

1. **Exact RL training compute cost** — the paper gives structural details but not total GPU-hours for Stage 2 RL training
2. **AlphaGo Master's architecture changes** — DeepMind did not publish a separate paper for AlphaGo Master; architectural improvements over AlphaGo Lee are not publicly detailed (aside from the move to 4 TPUs and the AlphaGo Zero paper's retrospective comparison)
3. **Game 4 "divine move" full analysis** — Aja Huang's conference explanation (July 2016) is in Chinese; no peer-reviewed analysis exists of exactly how move 78 caused the policy network failure
4. **λ hyperparameter sensitivity** — the mixing weight between value network and rollout (λ = 0.5) is reported but ablation sensitivity is only partially covered in extended data
